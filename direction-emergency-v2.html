SPECIAL EMERGENCY EDITION · WIRED FROM 276 N PALM CANYON DR.

Bread crisis hits Palm Canyon Drive;
twenty-four suspects identified.

Witnesses describe symptoms of "drooling" and "time loss" near 276 North Palm Canyon. Authorities recommend Dutch Crunch, applied liberally.

By Dena Banes · staff sandwich correspondent
— filed at the counter, midday rush.

For nineteen years now, the same small storefront in the Henry Frank Arcade has been baking the same Dutch Crunch loaf, and the same line has been forming around the corner just before lunch. The line is not a problem. The line is the lead.

"I've been here since 2007," said Dena Banes, the shop's longtime catering desk. "I bake the bread before you wake up. The sandwiches name themselves." Banes declined to comment further on what she called "the cheeky ones."

The shop's twenty-four specialty sandwiches are each named after either a tennis legend or a Palm Springs cultural icon — Sinatra, Hope, Manilow, the Federer. Sources confirm prices have remained stable at $14.75.

Authorities at (760) 778-7900 can be reached for further comment, but most callers report being immediately distracted by the menu. The Gazette will continue to monitor the situation.

"Worth the wait. Worth the line. Worth the entire afternoon."

SECTION B · BREAD WATCH

Field Guide: the loaf, in cross-section.

— a public-service report.

Dutch Crunch — the Northern California staple — gets its name from the cracked tortoise-shell crust formed by a rice-flour paste applied just before baking. Gazette sources confirm Banes mixes the paste at sunrise, bakes by 8am, and at any given moment the loaf you are holding is no more than a few hours old.

It is the only sandwich-bread in Palm Springs that requires both hands and a paper bag. Crust shatters on contact. Crumb is roughly thirty percent air. Tomato slides slightly. The Gazette's science desk has confirmed: this is correct. This is the design.

If you don't pick Dutch Crunch we will judge you (lovingly).
